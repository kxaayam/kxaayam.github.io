Positionality in Engineering Design Process

Engineering design to me is...

...a way of creating possibilities for people. Through systems, structures, and technologies, engineers shape how people live, move, communicate, and experience the world. Good engineering design can expand access, connection, safety, and opportunity. But these possibilities are never created in unlimited space.

Engineering design always happens within constraints. However, even within the same constraints, engineers may make different design decisions. Stakeholder needs and practical limitations narrow the design space, but they do not determine one obvious solution. Engineers still choose how to frame the problem, what to prioritize, and which tradeoffs are acceptable. These decisions are shaped by positionality, because personal values and lived experiences influence how a designer interprets the problem in the first place.

My Positionality: Values and Lived Perspective

I see functionality as the baseline requirement of a design outcome. Beyond that baseline, innovation is a value I prioritize in the design process. As a first-year engineering student, I see design as a process of learning, so I am drawn to approaches that allow me to explore new ideas, develop new skills, and expand the tools I can use to respond to a problem.

My lived experience also shapes the way I think about design. Having grown up in two countries and three cities, I have been exposed to different cultural and social environments, which has made me more aware that people in seemingly similar roles or categories can still experience the world very differently. Because of this, I do not want to understand stakeholders only by label; I want to understand the context that shapes their needs, expectations, and experiences. Together, these values and experiences influence how I interpret design problems and what I pay attention to when making decisions.

How This Shapes My Design Approach

1. Innovation and a thorough understanding of the problem shape the way I begin the design process. In the early stages, I rely on both secondary and primary research to develop a stronger understanding of the problem space. Secondary research allows me to examine existing concepts, precedents, and possible directions, while primary research helps me understand stakeholder needs and how the problem is experienced in practice. Together, these forms of research allow me to narrow the design space in a more informed, deliberate, and context-sensitive way.

2. I also try to understand stakeholders in context rather than only by category. Engineers often design for specific stakeholder groups, but those stakeholders are not defined only by a label. Their needs are shaped by culture, environment, and lived experience. Because of this, I believe design should go beyond identifying who stakeholders are and try to understand the specific conditions in which they experience the problem.

Biases and Limitations

My perspective on engineering design is still developing, and I recognize that I bring certain biases to the design process. Because I value context and multiple perspectives, I sometimes spend more time understanding and framing a problem before moving toward a solution. This can support a more thoughtful interpretation of the design space, but it can also slow decision-making. I may also focus more on conceptual possibilities than on practical constraints such as feasibility, efficiency, or simplicity. Recognizing these tendencies helps me approach design with greater awareness and balance.
